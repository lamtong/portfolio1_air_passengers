Portfolio Task 1: Time Series Forecasting with the Decomposition Technique¶

Introduction¶

Carry out a study on behalf of a business analytics specialised consultancy on a subsample of monthly data. Provided is a common starter time series data set which shows the number of airline passengers there were per month in the United States in the 1950s.

Key Findings¶

  • The number of Air Passengers during the 1959 - 1960 period depicts an upward trends. And 1960 is predicted to witness the highest amount of customers.
  • There are seasonality factors in the passenger number trend with 12 seasons, with a similar pattern repeats annually.
  • July and August were usually the months with highest demand for air travelling, while November witnessed the lowest.

Recommendations¶

  • Increase Capacity During Peak Months July and August: The Airline should consider increasing flight capacity during these months. This could involve adding more flights or larger aircraft to meet the surge in demand.

  • Since November shows the lowest demand, airlines could focus on marketing strategies to attract more customers during this period. Promotions, special offers, and targeted advertising could be used to stimulate interest in off-peak travel.

  • With a clear seasonal trend of 12 seasons, airlines could optimise their scheduling to accommodate fluctuations in demand. By understanding these patterns, airlines can better plan routes and flight frequencies, reducing costs during off-peak periods and enhancing efficiency during peak seasons.

2. How many seasons can be recognised in this data set?¶

There are 12 seasons, as according tho the graph in Question 1 monthly air passengers typically follow an annual cycle where patterns repeat every 12 months. So, the seasonality period is 12 months, meaning one full cycle patterns repeat every year.

The result of the Autocorrelation Function method below also confirms this.

The 16-month Centered Moving Average (CMA) for 11 years data (1949-1959):¶

With 11-year monthly data, the window of the CMA should be large enough to smoothly capture the trend but small enough to keep the details for optimal prediction later.

After trying different window values for the CMA, 16 seems to be an appropriate value. Which results in minimal fitting and testing errors.

Calculating Seasonal Components Values:¶

1. Typical Seasonal Factors:¶

For the Multiplicative Time Series Model:

$$\text{Number of Passengers} = \text{Trend} \times \text{Seasonal}$$

With:

  • Seasonal is the Typical Seasonal Factors of each month:
$$\text{Seasonal} = \text{Correction Factor} \times \text{Average Seasonal Values}$$$$\text{Correction Factor} = \frac{ \text{Number of Seasons}}{\sum \text{Average Seasonal Factors}}$$
In [39]:
# Typical seasonal factor calculation
df_his['calendar_month'] = df_his.index.astype(str).str[-5:-3] # extract the month value
df_60['calendar_month'] = df_60.index.astype(str).str[-5:-3] # extract the month value
avg_seasonal = df_his.groupby('calendar_month')['s'].mean() # monthly seasonal factor averages
avg_seasonal = avg_seasonal.to_frame()
avg_seasonal.rename(columns={'s':'s_mean'}, inplace = True)

# correction factor
correct_ft = 12/sum(avg_seasonal['s_mean'])

# Calculate Typical seasonal factors
avg_seasonal['typ_S'] = avg_seasonal['s_mean'] * correct_ft

6. Calculate the Mean Absolute Error and Mean Square Error for the forecasts.¶

  • The fitted decomposition is fitted well with the historical data (1949 - 1959), with low Mean Absolute Error (12.29) and low Mean Squared Error (223.59). This means that the decomposition model generalises the historical data well.

  • Regarding the 1960 prediction: On average, the prediction misses the actual number of 1960 passengers by about 31.8. This means that, in absolute terms, the forecasted values are typically off by around 32 passengers.

  • The MSE (1149.1) is sensitive to larger errors, meaning there were likely some larger errors in specific months. After testing the prediction with different CMA windows, the value of 16 resulted in the lowest possible MAE and MSE combination for the 1960 prediction.

  • The higher errors in the 1960 predictions might due to other factors beyond the scope of the prediction using historical data, such as economic dynamic, market trend, etc.
